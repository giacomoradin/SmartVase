## 2. Component sheets (documentation and pitfalls)

### 2.1 HC-SR04: ultrasonic distance sensor (×6)

**Principle**: a **10 µs** trigger pulse causes an **8-cycle burst at 40 kHz**
to be emitted; the ECHO pin stays high for a time proportional to the
round-trip travel of the sound. Distance ≈ `t_echo / 58` µs/cm.

**Key specs** (datasheet): 5V supply, ~15 mA draw, 2 to 400 cm range (optimal
10 to 250 cm), ~3 mm resolution, **~15° usable cone**, ~20 Hz maximum polling
rate (≥ 50 to 60 ms between readings of the same probe).

**Known pitfalls**:
- **Blind zone under 2 cm** and unstable readings under 4 cm.
- **Soft** surfaces (fabric, foliage) or **very tilted** ones absorb or
  deflect the echo → missing readings. The lab floor is the easy case; the
  pot with the plant is not.
- **Cross-talk**: two probes listening to the same echo fool each other.
  The firmware reads **one probe every 30 ms in round-robin**, so two
  consecutive readings are 30 ms apart and the same probe is re-read every
  180 ms: the constraints are respected by construction.
- On out-of-range some clones keep ECHO high for up to ~200 ms: the driver
  (`Ultrasonic.cpp`) uses a timeout and returns `NAN`, and the re-read
  happens well after the echo has died out.

**How the firmware handles it**: EMA filter (alpha=0.4), valid range 2 to 200 cm
(120 for US4), 10 consecutive invalid readings → `NAN` → for US4 this
triggers the pump fail-safe.

**To verify tomorrow**: see tests T2/T3. If a probe always reads `NAN`:
trig/echo are swapped (compare against `PINS - Sheet1.csv`) or VCC/GND is missing.

Sources: [SparkFun datasheet](https://cdn.sparkfun.com/datasheets/Sensors/Proximity/HCSR04.pdf) ·
[Handsontec user guide](https://www.handsontec.com/dataspecs/HC-SR04-Ultrasonic.pdf) ·
[Adafruit guide](https://cdn-learn.adafruit.com/downloads/pdf/ultrasonic-sonar-distance-sensors.pdf)

### 2.2 DS3232: I²C Real-Time Clock

**Principle**: RTC with a **temperature-compensated TCXO** oscillator:
**±2 ppm** accuracy (~ ±1 minute/year). I²C address **0x68**. The DS3232
adds SRAM compared to the DS3231; for us they are equivalent.

**Backup battery**: a **CR2032** keeps the time while the device is off
(backup draw ~3 uA -> 3 to 10 years of life).

**The flag we care about: OSF** (Oscillator Stop Flag, bit 7 of status
register 0x0F): it is 1 if the oscillator has stopped since the time was
last set -> **the time read is not reliable**. Typical of a new module or
one with a dead/absent battery. The firmware reads it (`rtc` in the CLI) and
`rtc set <epoch>` clears it by writing the time.

**To verify tomorrow**: is the battery present? `rtc` → `time_valid`; after
`rtc set`, **remove power for 30 s** and re-check that the time advances
(backup-battery test, T6).

Sources: [DS3231 datasheet (Analog Devices)](https://www.analog.com/media/en/technical-documentation/data-sheets/ds3231.pdf) ·
[module guide](https://easyelecmodule.com/ultimate-ds3231-rtc-module/)

### 2.3 Fork: resistive soil-moisture sensor

**Principle**: two probes measure the soil's **conductivity**: more
water -> lower resistance -> (on YL-69/FC-28-type modules with an analog
output) **lower AO voltage** -> lower ADC value. Typical values: dry/air
close to **1023**, submerged in water **~200 to 400**.

Note: **Watch the direction**: this holds for the standard topology of
commercial modules. If our fork is wired on a "home-made" divider the
direction might be inverted -> **must be measured, not assumed** (T4).
The firmware default `soil_dry_threshold = 450` assumes "dry = low value"
and is probably **to be inverted or re-calibrated** after the test.

**Known pitfall: electrolysis corrosion**: with the probes continuously
powered, the metal corrodes (visible degradation in 6 to 12 months). The
firmware currently reads the ADC continuously with the fork always
powered at 5V: **future TODO**: power the fork from a GPIO and turn it on
only for the reading (a few ms every minute), extending the sensor's life
by years.

**To verify tomorrow**: T4 (three values: air / water / damp soil) and
write down the direction. Also check whether there is an LM393 comparator
module in between (in that case use the **AO** output, not DO).

Sources: [Random Nerd Tutorials YL-69](https://randomnerdtutorials.com/guide-for-soil-moisture-sensor-yl-69-or-hl-69-with-the-arduino/) ·
[YL-69 datasheet](https://www.electronicoscaldas.com/datasheet/YL-69-HL-69.pdf) ·
[FC-28 guide](https://maker.pro/arduino/projects/arduino-soil-moisture-sensor)

### 2.4 Photoresistor (LDR, presumed GL5528)

**Principle**: resistance that drops with light. GL5528: **8 to 20 kΩ at 10 lux**,
**> 1 MΩ in the dark**. It is read with a divider (typically a fixed 10 kΩ).

**Signal direction** (to be determined, T5):
- LDR towards **VCC** (fixed resistor towards GND): more light → **higher**
  ADC (more common topology, gives ~1.6 to 2.5 V indoors).
- LDR towards **GND**: more light → **lower** ADC.

The firmware default `light_threshold = 600` and the light/shadow-seek
logic (`Movement.cpp`: turns right if `lux < threshold` in LIGHT mode)
assume "more light = higher value". **If the divider is inverted, the
robot will seek darkness while believing it is seeking light**: this is
why T5 is mandatory before trying `mode light`.

Sources: [GL5528 specs](https://www.devobox.com/en/photosensors/9-photoresistor-gl5528-ldr.html) ·
[LDR divider guide](https://blog.udemy.com/arduino-ldr/)

### 2.5 Motor driver (Pololu Dual VNH5019 Motor Driver Shield `ash02b`, 2014)

**Confirmed** (no longer L298N): a dual shield with two **VNH5019** chips,
5.5 to 24 V, ~12 A continuous per channel. Note: Interface **different from the L298N**.

**Principle**: 4 signals per motor: `INA`/`INB` (direction), `PWM`
(speed), `EN/DIAG` (enable + fault flag). Truth table: `INA=1,
INB=0, PWM=high, EN=1` → forward; `INA=0, INB=1` → backward; `INA=INB` → brake.
`EN/DIAG` is open-drain with a pull-up on the shield (R1/R10 4.7k → VDD): at
rest HIGH = driver **enabled**; the chip pulls it LOW on a fault (overtemp/
overcurrent/undervoltage).

**Specs and pitfalls**:
- **Common GND Mega↔shield mandatory**: without a ground reference the
  logic signals have no return path → outputs at 0V even with VDD present.
  This is cause #1 of "the code runs but the motors don't move".
- **Logic VDD** ~5 V: the shield can generate it from its own regulator
  (VOUT, "ARDVIN=VOUT" jumper); in that case **do not** also connect the
  Mega's 5V.
- **EN A=B**: the shield has a jumper to tie the chip's ENA/ENB together
  into a single EN line per motor: check it is closed.
- Pins in our wiring (CSV): M1PWM=D6, M1INA=D43, M1INB=D45, M1EN=D41
  (left); M2PWM=D7, M2INA=D47, M2INB=D49, M2EN=D39 (right). Note: On the
  standard shield pin 6 is M1EN, not M1PWM: make sure the wire from D6
  really goes to **M1PWM**. Which channel is the left wheel → verify on the bench (T7).
- The firmware (`Movement.cpp`) reads EN/DIAG as `INPUT_PULLUP`: `diag` shows
  `fault L/R`. EN is optional (wiring it is only needed for fault diagnostics).

Sources: [Pololu Dual VNH5019 Shield](https://www.pololu.com/product/2507) ·
[VNH5019 datasheet](https://www.pololu.com/file/0J504/vnh5019.pdf) ·
[Pololu user's guide / library](https://www.pololu.com/docs/0J49)

### 2.6 5V relay module (pump)

**Principle**: the GPIO drives (usually via an optocoupler) the coil of
an electromechanical relay (typically Songle **SRD-05VDC-SL-C**, contacts
rated up to 10 A / 30 V DC). The load goes on the **COM + NO** (normally
open) contacts: relay at rest = pump off.

**Pitfalls**:
- **Trigger polarity**: optocoupler-based modules are almost always
  **active LOW** (GPIO LOW = relay ON), simple-transistor ones are often
  active high. The firmware assumes active-low (`PUMP_RELAY_ACTIVE_LOW 1`
  in `Pump.cpp`): test T8 confirms or disproves this in 10 seconds.
- **JD-VCC jumper**: if present and removed, the coil must be powered
  separately (galvanic isolation). With the jumper in place, the coil and
  logic share the 5V: fine for us, but the coil draws ~70 mA: also count
  this in the 5V budget.
- The relay coil already has its own diode on the module, but the **pump**
  (a DC motor) generates back-EMF spikes on the contacts: if there is no
  **flyback diode (e.g. 1N4007) in antiparallel across the pump**, add one
  (cathode to +).

Sources: [Circuit Basics relay](https://www.circuitbasics.com/setting-up-a-5v-relay-on-the-arduino/) ·
[SRD-05VDC datasheet](https://www.datasheetcafe.com/srd-05vdc-sl-c-datasheet-pdf/) ·
[JD-VCC explained](https://www.geya.net/5v-relay-module-how-it-works-and-application/)

### 2.7 Mini submersible pump (presumed 3 to 6V DC)

**Typical specs**: 3 to 6 V, 100 to 200 mA, ~40 to 110 cm head. Must never be
driven directly from a GPIO (~40 mA limit): in our design it goes through the relay.

**Pitfalls**:
- **Dry running**: small submersible pumps use the water to cool and
  lubricate themselves: running dry damages them quickly. This is exactly
  the reason for the **tank protection** implemented in the firmware
  (blocking on `tank_empty_cm` + auto-stop): test T9 validates it end-to-end.
- **Flyback diode** across the pump if missing (see 2.6).
- Priming: on first power-up it may take a few seconds for the water to
  fill the impeller → do the first test with `pump 5000`.

Sources: [mini pump guide](https://zbotic.in/mini-water-pump-guide-dc-submersible-peristaltic-pumps-for-arduino/) ·
[controlling a pump with a relay](https://www.hibit.dev/posts/128/how-to-control-mini-water-pump-with-arduino)

### 2.8 ESP32-CAM (AI-Thinker, OV2640)

**Power: cause #1 of the problems**: it needs **5V with at least
500 mA real**(2A recommended); with Wi-Fi + camera the current spikes
cause **brownouts** (spontaneous resets) if the power supply is weak
(e.g. taken from the FTDI). Remedy: dedicated 5V supply + **≥ 470 µF
electrolytic capacitor between 5V and GND** near the board. The firmware
disables the brownout detector at boot, but that is a band-aid: if T11
shows resets, it is the power supply.

**Flashing the firmware**: without an MB base you need a 3.3V FTDI and
**GPIO0 tied to GND during reset** to enter the bootloader; then
disconnect GPIO0 and reset again. GPIO4 drives the onboard flash LED
(not used by the firmware today).

**Camera**: the OV2640's flat cable is cause #1 of `Camera init
FAILED`: reseat it gently.

Sources: [Random Nerd Tutorials pinout](https://randomnerdtutorials.com/esp32-cam-ai-thinker-pinout/) ·
[brownout fix](https://www.makerguides.com/fix-brownout-of-esp32-cam/) ·
[DroneBot dev guide](https://dronebotworkshop.com/esp32-cam-develop/)
